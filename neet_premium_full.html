Genetics & Molecular Biology ~30 Qs · Days 4,5,7
Exact NCERT focus
  • Ch 5 (12th): Mendel's laws — read every experiment description word by word. NEET asks "who said what".
  • Ch 5: Punnett squares for dihybrid cross — practise drawing, not just reading.
  • Ch 5: Deviations table — codominance vs incomplete dominance — know examples (ABO blood group, snapdragon).
  • Ch 5: Chromosomal disorders — memorise chromosome number: Down's (47,+21), Turner's (45,XO), Klinefelter's (47,XXY).
  • Ch 6 (12th): DNA structure — Chargaff's rule, Watson-Crick model, B-DNA specifics.
  • Ch 6: Replication enzymes in order — Helicase → Primase → DNA Pol III → DNA Pol I → Ligase.
  • Ch 6: Transcription vs Translation — know which enzymes, where (nucleus vs ribosome), what is produced.
  • Ch 6: Lac operon — inducer, repressor, structural genes. Most repeated NEET topic in this chapter.
Key diagrams to draw from memory
  • DNA double helix with labelled bases, sugar-phosphate backbone, H-bonds
  • Replication fork with all enzymes labelled at correct positions
  • Lac operon — off state (repressor bound) vs on state (inducer present)
NCERT trick: The exact chromosome number in disorders is directly asked. Make a table: Disorder | Chromosome | Number | Key feature. Takes 10 minutes, earns 4+ marks.
MCQ trap: NEET often asks "which enzyme seals Okazaki fragments?" Answer: DNA Ligase (not Pol I). Know the difference between their roles.
Human Physiology ~25 Qs · Days 10,11
Exact NCERT focus
  • Ch 16 (11th): Digestion — enzyme + substrate + product table for each organ. Know pH of each region.
  • Ch 16: Villi structure, role of lacteals, hepatic portal vein path.
  • Ch 18 (11th): Cardiac cycle phases — systole, diastole, duration (0.8 sec total). Lub-dub sounds and cause.
  • Ch 18: Blood components — RBC lifespan (120 days), WBC types and functions, platelet count.
  • Ch 17 (11th): Breathing — tidal volume (500 mL), vital capacity (3400–4800 mL), residual volume (1200 mL). These numbers are directly asked.
  • Ch 19 (11th): Nephron diagram — PCT, Loop of Henle, DCT, collecting duct — what is reabsorbed where.
  • Ch 21 (11th): All endocrine glands — hormone name, target organ, hypo/hypersecretion disease. This is a table topic.
  • Ch 21: Neuron structure, resting potential (-70mV), action potential mechanism — Na+ in, K+ out.
NCERT trick: Make one master table for hormones: Gland | Hormone | Function | Deficiency disease. This single table covers 6–8 NEET questions every year.
MCQ trap: Students confuse "pulmonary" (lung) and "systemic" circulation directions. Pulmonary artery carries deoxygenated blood — counter-intuitive. Memorise as an exception.
Plant Physiology — Photosynthesis & Respiration ~12 Qs · Days 8,9
Exact NCERT focus (your weak area — extra detail)
  • Ch 13 (11th): Photosynthetic pigments — chlorophyll a, b, carotenoids, xanthophylls. Know absorption peaks.
  • Ch 13: Light reaction — PS II absorbs at 680nm, PS I at 700nm. Photolysis of water releases O2.
  • Ch 13: Calvin cycle inputs/outputs — 3 CO2 → 1 G3P. RuBisCO is the enzyme. 3 turns needed per G3P.
  • Ch 13: C4 plants — bundle sheath cells + mesophyll cells. Examples: maize, sugarcane, sorghum.
  • Ch 13: CAM plants — stomata open at night. Examples: cacti, agaves, pineapple.
  • Ch 14 (11th): Glycolysis — 10 steps not needed; know net ATP (2), NADH (2), pyruvate (2).
  • Ch 14: Krebs cycle — per glucose: 6 CO2, 8 NADH, 2 FADH2, 2 ATP (substrate-level).
  • Ch 14: Total ATP — 36–38 per glucose (aerobic). Fermentation = 2 ATP only.
The confusion fix — photosynthesis vs respiration
  • Draw both cycles side by side. Calvin cycle: CO2 IN → sugar OUT. Krebs: sugar IN → CO2 OUT.
  • Both use NADH but opposite directions — Calvin regenerates NADPH, Krebs produces NADH.
  • Location: Photosynthesis = chloroplast stroma (Calvin) + thylakoid (light rxn). Respiration = cytoplasm (glycolysis) + mitochondrial matrix (Krebs).
MCQ trap: "Where does O2 evolution occur?" — Thylakoid membrane (during photolysis), NOT stroma. Students write stroma and lose marks.
Cell Biology & Cell Division ~18 Qs · Days 1,2
Exact NCERT focus
  • Ch 8 (11th): Prokaryote vs eukaryote comparison table — wall composition, organelles, ribosome size (70S vs 80S).
  • Ch 8: All organelle functions — especially ER (smooth vs rough), Golgi (cis vs trans face), lysosomes ("suicide bags").
  • Ch 10 (11th): Mitosis — PMAT stages, what happens at each. Chromosome vs chromatid count at each stage.
  • Ch 10: Meiosis — crossing over in Prophase I (pachytene). Chiasmata formation. Terminal vs localised.
  • Ch 10: Significance of meiosis — genetic variation, haploid gametes. NEET asks this conceptually.
MCQ trap: Ribosome size — 80S in cytoplasm of eukaryotes but 70S in chloroplasts and mitochondria of eukaryotes. This is a classic NEET trick question.
Classification (Animal + Plant Kingdom) ~10 Qs · Day 16 · Your weak area
Strategy — do NOT read deeply. Use tables only.
  • Animal Kingdom: Learn basis of classification first (body plan, symmetry, coelom, notochord).
  • For each phylum: 1 distinguishing feature + 2 examples only. That is enough for NEET.
  • High-yield phyla: Porifera, Cnidaria, Platyhelminthes, Nematoda, Annelida, Arthropoda, Mollusca, Echinodermata, Chordata.
  • Plant Kingdom: Focus on alternation of generations pattern. Know dominant phase for each group.
  • Plant groups table: Thallophyta → Bryophyta → Pteridophyta → Gymnosperms → Angiosperms. Key feature of each + 1 example.
Time-saving rule: Spend maximum 90 minutes on ALL of classification. Use only NCERT summary boxes and tables at end of chapters. Do not re-read paragraphs.
Ecology ~15 Qs · Day 14
  • Ch 13,14 (12th): Ecosystem — producers, consumers, decomposers. Energy flow: 10% law (Lindemann).
  • Biogeochemical cycles — N cycle (bacteria names: Nitrosomonas, Nitrobacter, Rhizobium, Pseudomonas). These names are directly asked.
  • Population interactions table — mutualism, commensalism, parasitism, predation, competition — know example for each.
  • Ecological succession — primary vs secondary, climax community concept.
  • Biodiversity — hotspots in India (Western Ghats, Eastern Himalayas). In-situ vs ex-situ conservation with examples.
High-yield: Bacteria names in N-cycle are asked almost every year. Nitrification (Nitrosomonas → Nitrobacter), Denitrification (Pseudomonas). Make flashcards for these.
Biotechnology ~12 Qs · Day 13
  • Ch 11 (12th): Tools of recombinant DNA — restriction enzymes (EcoRI, HindIII), vectors (plasmids, bacteriophages).
  • PCR steps — denaturation (94°C) → annealing (~55°C) → extension (72°C, Taq polymerase). Temperatures are asked.
  • Gel electrophoresis — smaller fragments move faster, farther. Ethidium bromide stain.
  • Ch 12 (12th): Bt toxin — cry genes, cry1Ac (bollworm), cry2Ab (bollworm), cry1Ab (corn borer).
  • ADA deficiency — first gene therapy case. Know the cell type targeted (T-lymphocytes) and enzyme (adenosine deaminase).
  • ELISA, DNA fingerprinting — principle and application. Not mechanism depth.
MCQ trap: EcoRI cuts between G and AATTC — it creates sticky ends. HindIII also creates sticky ends. PVUII creates blunt ends. This distinction is frequently tested.
The 5-step daily study method — use this every single session
1
Survey (5 min): Open NCERT chapter. Read only headings, subheadings, bold words, and summary. Get the skeleton before the flesh. This primes your brain to absorb better.
2
Active read (45–60 min): Read line by line. Every time you see a number, name, or definition — underline it. Every time you see a comparison — put a bracket and write "compare" in margin. Every diagram — trace it with your finger, then copy it in rough on paper.
3
Close and recall (10 min): Close the book. Open blank notebook. Write everything you remember — facts, diagrams, tables. No peeking. This is the single most powerful retention tool known to science. It feels uncomfortable — that discomfort is learning.
4
MCQ sprint (20–25 min): Solve 20 PYQs on today's topic. Do not check answers during solving. After finishing — mark, check, and for every wrong answer: find the NCERT line that gives the correct answer. Log it in your mistake notebook.
5
Night flash review (10 min): Before sleeping, review 5 key facts from today without notes. Say them aloud. This triggers memory consolidation during sleep — it is not optional.
How to tackle diagrams (your biggest scoring opportunity)
Step 1 — Copy first
Copy the NCERT diagram exactly. Label every part. Use a pencil. This forces your visual memory to engage.
Step 2 — Draw from memory
Close book. Draw from scratch. Compare with original. Missing labels = weak spots. Repeat only those parts.
Step 3 — Attach a question
For each labelled part, write one question. "What does the Bundle of His do?" Answer from memory.
Step 4 — Revise at Day 3 and Day 7
Redraw the diagram again on Day 3, then Day 7. By Day 7 it should take under 2 minutes. That is exam-ready.
How to handle panic in exam (your #1 problem)
The 3-second rule
If you read a question and feel panic: count 3 seconds slowly. Re-read only the last line of the question (it tells you what is being asked). Eliminate 2 options first. Then decide between the remaining 2. Never re-read the whole question in panic mode — it wastes time and amplifies confusion.
Build exam speed now — not on exam day
In every MCQ practice session: set a timer. 40 seconds per question maximum. When the alarm goes off, mark your best guess and move on. Do this daily from Day 1. By Day 12 your brain will be conditioned to decide fast. Exam panic comes from unfamiliarity with time pressure — eliminate it in practice.

Active recall flashcards — test yourself without looking
Read the question. Think. Then tap "Show answer" to check.
What enzyme catalyses the first step of replication — unwinding the DNA double helix?
Helicase. It breaks hydrogen bonds between bases. Remember the order: Helicase → Primase → DNA Pol III → DNA Pol I → Ligase.
Name the enzyme that fixes CO2 in the Calvin cycle. What is the first stable product of C3 photosynthesis?
RuBisCO (Ribulose bisphosphate carboxylase-oxygenase). First stable product = 3-phosphoglycerate (3-PGA), a 3-carbon compound.
What is the chromosome number and karyotype in Turner's syndrome? What are two key clinical features?
45 chromosomes, karyotype 45,XO. Features: female phenotype, short stature, sterile (ovaries absent/streak ovaries), no secondary sex characters at puberty.
In the cardiac cycle, what causes the 'lub' sound and what causes the 'dub' sound?
'Lub' = closure of mitral (bicuspid) + tricuspid valves at the start of ventricular systole. 'Dub' = closure of aortic + pulmonary semilunar valves at the end of ventricular systole.
What are the three C4 plants given in NCERT? Why are C4 plants more efficient than C3 in hot conditions?
C4 plants: maize (corn), sugarcane, sorghum. More efficient because: CO2 is first fixed in mesophyll cells as a 4-carbon compound (OAA) and pumped to bundle sheath cells, concentrating CO2 around RuBisCO — this suppresses photorespiration.
What is the role of Nitrosomonas and Nitrobacter in the nitrogen cycle? What is the process called?
Nitrification: Nitrosomonas converts NH3 → NO2⁻. Nitrobacter converts NO2⁻ → NO3⁻. Together they convert ammonium to nitrate, making nitrogen available to plants.
What is the net ATP yield of aerobic respiration per glucose? How is it distributed (substrate-level vs oxidative phosphorylation)?
36–38 ATP total. Substrate-level phosphorylation: 4 ATP (2 glycolysis + 2 Krebs). Oxidative phosphorylation (ETC): 32–34 ATP from NADH and FADH2. Fermentation produces only 2 ATP (no ETC).
Name the vector used in recombinant DNA technology. What are the three essential components a vector must have?
Common vectors: plasmids (e.g., pBR322), bacteriophages. Three essential components: (1) Origin of replication (ori), (2) Selectable marker (antibiotic resistance gene), (3) Restriction enzyme recognition site (cloning site).
In meiosis, at which specific sub-stage does crossing over occur? What structure forms at the crossover point?
Crossing over occurs during Pachytene (sub-stage of Prophase I of Meiosis I). The structure formed at the crossover point is called a chiasma (plural: chiasmata).
What are the three PCR temperature steps and their purposes?
1. Denaturation: ~94°C — separates DNA strands by breaking H-bonds. 2. Annealing: ~55°C — primers bind to complementary sequences. 3. Extension: 72°C — Taq polymerase synthesises new strand from primers.
